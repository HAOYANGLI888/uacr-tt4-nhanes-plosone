# Results draft

## NHANES 2007-2012 discovery cohort

The pooled NHANES 2007-2012 files included 30442 records. After restricting the analysis to adults, excluding pregnant participants, requiring valid UACR and common thyroid indicators, excluding thyroid-medication users, and requiring complete cohort-construction covariates, 6487 participants remained in the discovery cohort.

Higher UACR was associated with higher TT4. In the fully adjusted survey-weighted model, each one-unit increase in natural-log UACR was associated with a 0.077-unit increase in TT4 (95% CI 0.035 to 0.119; P = 0.001; FDR-adjusted P = 0.010). Clinical UACR categories showed the same direction of association. Compared with UACR <30 mg/g, the adjusted TT4 difference was 0.228 (95% CI 0.032 to 0.425; P = 0.031) for UACR 30-300 mg/g and 0.412 (95% CI 0.127 to 0.696; P = 0.009) for UACR >=300 mg/g, with evidence of a positive trend (P for trend = 0.005).

## TT4 robustness analyses

The UACR-TT4 association remained directionally stable across prespecified analyses. All seven natural-log UACR sensitivity models yielded positive estimates and nominal P values below 0.05. The association remained evident after excluding participants with eGFR <60 mL/min/1.73 m2, diabetes, hypertension, UACR >=300 mg/g, or UACR values outside the 1st-99th percentile range, and after restriction to euthyroid participants. Restricted cubic spline analysis supported an overall association (P = 0.00463) without evidence of non-linearity (P = 0.518).

TGAb and TPOAb were retained as exploratory thyroid-autoimmunity outcomes. They did not provide a stable primary signal and were not used to define the central manuscript conclusion.

## NHANES III replication assessment

The NHANES III replication cohort included 11302 participants after prespecified exclusions. Using harmonized covariate adjustment, the association between natural-log UACR and TT4 was not statistically replicated in NHANES III. The H3 estimate was close to zero (beta = 0.003, 95% CI -0.074 to 0.080; P = 0.932; n = 11200). The NHANES III clinical-category analysis likewise did not support a positive trend (P for trend = 0.541). A cohort-interaction analysis indicated heterogeneity between the NHANES 2007-2012 and NHANES III estimates (P = 0.00268).

## Mortality extension

Among the 6487 discovery-cohort participants, 6484 were eligible for mortality follow-up. During follow-up, 897 all-cause deaths and 249 cardiovascular deaths were identified.

Natural-log UACR was associated with both mortality outcomes. In the fully adjusted survey-weighted Cox model, the hazard ratio (HR) was 1.387 for all-cause mortality (95% CI 1.285 to 1.498; P < 0.001) and 1.331 for cardiovascular mortality (95% CI 1.161 to 1.526; P < 0.001). TT4 showed a modest association with all-cause mortality (HR 1.065 per unit increase, 95% CI 1.009 to 1.125; P = 0.022) but not cardiovascular mortality (HR 1.108, 95% CI 0.978 to 1.255; P = 0.108). When TT4 was standardized, each standard-deviation increase was associated with all-cause mortality (HR 1.103, 95% CI 1.014 to 1.200; P = 0.022) but not cardiovascular mortality (HR 1.173, 95% CI 0.965 to 1.424; P = 0.108).

The primary UACR mortality associations remained positive after excluding deaths within the first two years of follow-up and after restriction to euthyroid participants. After excluding deaths within two years, the HRs for natural-log UACR were 1.377 (95% CI 1.265 to 1.499) for all-cause mortality and 1.303 (95% CI 1.127 to 1.507) for cardiovascular mortality. In euthyroid participants, the corresponding HRs were 1.388 (95% CI 1.278 to 1.508) and 1.294 (95% CI 1.122 to 1.492).

Secondary joint-category models did not show a monotonic increase across the four UACR-TT4 groups. No evidence of multiplicative effect modification was observed for natural-log UACR x TT4 or for albuminuria category x high-TT4 group. These joint-category analyses therefore support cautious descriptive interpretation rather than a combined-risk claim.

## Supplementary exploratory MR analysis

The exploratory MR module did not directly evaluate UACR -> TT4 because FT4 and TT4 GWAS were unavailable in the searchable OpenGWAS index. UACR and albuminuria each had only one LD-clumped genome-wide significant instrument. The multi-SNP eGFR -> TSH analysis did not reach statistical significance after FDR correction (IVW beta = -0.885, 95% CI -1.898 to 0.127; P = 0.087; FDR-adjusted P = 0.260). The TSH -> eGFR estimate was statistically significant but was based on a single SNP and a TSH protein proxy; it is reported as an exploratory Supplementary finding only.
